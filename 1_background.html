Background Information on the Philosophy of Science


Ray D. Bradbury, “The Sound of Thunder” (1952) 🔗

  • This short story is about a man that travels millions of years into the past and steps on a butterfly by accident. When he goes back to his present, he discovers that a totally new and radically different political system is in place.
  • It is mentioned by De Millo, Lipton, and Perlis in their article to illustrate how small changes in a program can produce unexpected and even chaotic results.

Thomas S. Kuhn, The Structure of Scientific Revolutions (1962) 🔗

  • The book focuses on the context of the Scientific Revolution in Early Modernity with the adoption of Copernicus’ astronomical model.
  • In this text Kuhn elaborated the concept of paradigm. A paradigm is a set of rules, theories, principles, methodologies shared by a (scientific) community.
  • Thus, what characterizes the Scientific Revolution is not that it turned science into an accurate and truthful field, but that the system employed before became insufficient to provide answers for many questions that were being raised and new paradigms started to be considered.
  • Fetzer mentions this author as an example of a certain perspective in philosophy that “emphasizes the social dimension in scientific communities.” He implies that De Millo et al. are taking this same position.
  • De Millo, Lipton, and Perlis mention this book to illustrate how even famous and well established theorems like Euler’s formula went through several transformations after their initial conception.

Karl R. Popper, Conjectures and Refutations: The Growth of Scientific Knowledge (1963) 🔗

  • Popper was a prominent philosopher of science in the 20th century and one of the main influences for Kuhn, Lakatos, and Feyerabend.
  • Some of his positions could be described as objectivist, he thinks that there can be accurate descriptions of facts.
  • In this sense, some scientific theories can be more verosimile than others. And this level of certainty or truthfulness can be measured.
  • For Popper, it is in the interplay between the tentative theories (conjectures) and error elimination (refutation) that scientific knowledge advances toward greater and greater problems.

Imre Lakatos, Proofs and Refutations: The Logic of Mathematical Discovery (1976) 🔗

  • It was written as a Socratic dialogue.
  • In this book, Lakatos established that no theorem is totally complete, that they are not a representation of a true fact, but just an explanation with no solid counterexample yet found.
  • Before publishing this book, Lakatos introduced in 1970 the concept of "research programme." With this, he pointed out that science is constituted by a community of people who share some principles and interests. It is by sharing these elements that research on a specific field is done and new theories emerge, either validating, expanding, or contradicting the principles that support this community.

Paul K. Feyerabend, Against Method: Outline of an Anarchistic Theory of Knowledge (1975) 🔗

  • Feyerabend took Lakatos’ and Kuhn’s considerations one step further and argued that what determines what research programme or paradigm should be followed is propaganda.
  • For Feyerabend, science is guided by stylistic elements such as attractive theories, simple arguments, or elegant expressions.
